Palpation of Testes
The soft, pliable skin of the scrotum makes it easy to 
palpate the testes and the structures related to them 
(e.g., the epididymis and ductus deferens). The left tes-
tis commonly lies at a more inferior level than does the right one.
Hypospadias
Hypospadias is a common congenital anomaly of the 
penis, occurring in 1 in 300 newborns. In the simplest 
and most common form, glanular hypospadias, the 
external urethral oriﬁ
 ce is on the ventral aspect of the glans 
penis. In other infants, the defect is in the body of the penis 
(penile hypospadias) (Fig. B3.33A), or in the perineum (peno-
scrotal or scrotal hypospadias) (Fig. B3.33B). Hence, the 
external urethral oriﬁ
 ce is on the urethral surface of the penis. 
The embryological basis of penile and penoscrotal hypospadias 
is failure of the urogenital folds to fuse on the ventral surface 
of the penis, completing the formation of the spongy urethra. 
It is believed that hypospadias is associated with an inadequate 
production of androgens by the fetal testes. Differences in the 
timing and degree of hormonal insufﬁ
 ciency probably account 
for the different types of hypospadias.
