The medial, triangular ulnar collateral ligament 
extends from the medial epicondyle of the humerus to the 
coronoid process and olecranon of the ulna and consists of 
three bands: (1) the anterior cord-like band is the strongest, 
(2) the posterior fan-like band is the weakest, and (3) the 
slender oblique band deepens the socket for the trochlea of 
the humerus.
MOVEMENTS OF ELBOW JOINT
Flexion and extension occur at the elbow joint. The long axis 
of the fully extended ulna makes an angle of approximately 
170° with the long axis of the humerus. This carrying angle 
(Fig. 6.99) is named for the way the forearm angles away 
from the body when something is carried, such as a pail of 
water. The obliquity of the ulna and thus of the carrying 
angle is more pronounced (the angle is approximately 10° 
more acute) in women than in men. It is teleologically said 
to enable the swinging limbs to clear the wide female pel-
vis when walking. In the anatomical position, the elbow is 
against the waist. The carrying angle disappears when the 
forearm is pronated.
