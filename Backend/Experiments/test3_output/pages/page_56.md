3. The articulating structures of cartilaginous joints are 
united by hyaline cartilage or ﬁ
 brocartilage. In primary 
cartilaginous joints, or synchondroses, the bones are 
united by hyaline cartilage, which permits slight bending 
during early life. Primary cartilaginous joints are usually 
temporary unions, such as those present during the devel-
opment of a long bone (Figs. I.14 and I.16C), where the bony 
epiphysis and the shaft are joined by an epiphysial plate. 
Primary cartilaginous joints permit growth in the length of 
a bone. When full growth is achieved, the epiphysial plate 
converts to bone and the epiphyses fuse with the diaphy-
sis. Secondary cartilaginous joints, or symphyses, are 
strong, slightly movable joints united by ﬁ
 brocartilage. 
The ﬁ
 brocartilaginous intervertebral discs (Fig. I.16C) 
between the vertebrae consist of binding connective tis-
sue that joins the vertebrae together. Cumulatively, these 
joints provide strength and shock absorption as well as 
considerable ﬂ
 exibility to the vertebral column (spine).
