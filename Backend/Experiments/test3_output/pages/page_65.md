Muscle Dysfunction and Paralysis
From the clinical perspective, it is important not 
only to think in terms of the action normally pro-
duced by a given muscle but also to consider what 
loss of function would occur if the muscle failed to function 
(paralysis). How would the dysfunction of a given muscle or 
muscle group be manifest (i.e., what are the visible signs)?
Absence of Muscle Tone
Although a gentle force, muscle tone can have 
important effects: the tonus of muscles in the lips 
helps keep the teeth aligned, for instance. When 
this gentle but constant pressure is absent (due to paralysis 
or a short lip that leaves the teeth exposed), teeth migrate, 
becoming everted (“buck teeth”).
The absence of muscle tone in an unconscious patient 
(e.g., under a general anesthetic) may allow joints to be dislo-
cated as he or she is being lifted or positioned. When a muscle 
is denervated (loses its nerve supply), it becomes paralyzed
