Development of Cranium
The bones of the calvaria and some parts of the cranial 
base develop by intramembranous ossiﬁ
 cation. Most 
parts of the cranial base develop by endochondral 
ossiﬁ
 cation. At birth, the bones of the calvaria are smooth and 
unilaminar; no diploë is present. The frontal and parietal emi-
nences are especially prominent (Fig. B7.6). The cranium of a 
neonate is disproportionately large compared to other parts of 
the skeleton; however, the facial aspect is small compared to 
the calvaria, which forms approximately one eighth of the cra-
nium. In the adult, the facial skeleton forms one third of the 
cranium. The large size of the calvaria in infants results from 
precocious growth and development of the brain and eyes.
