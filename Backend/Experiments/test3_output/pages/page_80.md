Spinal Nerves. Spinal nerves initially arise from the spi-
nal cord as rootlets (a detail commonly omitted from dia-
grams for the sake of simplicity); the rootlets converge to 
form two nerve roots (Fig. I.34):
1. An anterior (ventral) nerve root, consisting of motor 
(efferent) ﬁ
 bers passing from nerve cell bodies in the 
anterior horn of spinal cord gray matter to effector organs 
located peripherally.
2. A posterior (dorsal) nerve root, consisting of sensory 
(afferent) ﬁ
 bers from cell bodies in the spinal (sensory) or 
posterior (dorsal) root ganglion (commonly abbreviated in 
clinical use as “DRG”) that extend peripherally to sensory 
endings and centrally to the posterior horn of spinal cord 
gray matter.
The posterior and anterior nerve roots unite, within or just 
proximal to the intervertebral foramen, to form a mixed (both 
motor and sensory) spinal nerve, which immediately divides 
into two rami (L., branches): a posterior (dorsal) ramus and 
an anterior (ventral) ramus. As branches of the mixed spi-
nal nerve, the posterior and anterior rami carry both motor 
and sensory ﬁ
