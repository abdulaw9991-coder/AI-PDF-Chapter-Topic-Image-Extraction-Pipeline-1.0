Radiating laterally from the incisive papilla are several 
parallel transverse palatine folds or rugae (Fig. 7.85). 
These folds assist with manipulation of food during mastica-
tion. Passing posteriorly in the midline of the palate from 
the incisive papilla is a narrow whitish streak, the palatine 
raphe. It may present as a ridge anteriorly and a groove pos-
teriorly. The palatine raphe marks the site of fusion of the 
embryonic palatal processes (palatal shelves) (Moore et al. 
2012). You can feel the transverse palatine folds and the pala-
tine raphe with your tongue.
MUSCLES OF SOFT PALATE
The soft palate may be elevated so that it is in contact with 
the posterior wall of the pharynx. This closes the isthmus 
of the pharynx, requiring that one breathes through the 
mouth. The soft palate may also be drawn inferiorly so that it 
is in contact with the posterior part of the tongue. This closes 
the isthmus of the fauces, so that expired air passes through 
the nose (even when the mouth is open) and prevents sub-
stances in the oral cavity from passing to the pharynx. Tens-
ing the soft palate pulls it tight at an intermediate level so that 
the tongue may push against it, compressing masticated food 
and propelling it into the pharynx for swallowing.
