The brachial fascia, a sheath of deep fascia, encloses the 
arm like a snug sleeve deep to the skin and subcutaneous tis-
sue (Figs. 6.13A and 6.14A & B). It is continuous superiorly 
with the deltoid, pectoral, axillary, and infraspinous fascias. 
The brachial fascia is attached inferiorly to the epicondyles of 
the humerus and the olecranon of the ulna. This fascia is con-
tinuous with the antebrachial fascia, the deep fascia of the 
forearm. Two inter 
muscular septa—the medial and lateral 
intermuscular septa—extend from the deep surface of

supra-epicondylar ridges of the humerus (Fig. 6.14B). These 
intermuscular septa divide the arm into anterior (ﬂ

common innervation. The fascial compartments of the upper 
limb are important clinically because they also contain and 
direct the spread of infection or hemorrhage in the limb.
